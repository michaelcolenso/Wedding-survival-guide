## 3.1 The Booking Order — and Why It Matters

There is a specific order in which wedding vendors should be booked. Deviate from it and you will either (a) book something that doesn't work with something else, or (b) lose access to the best vendors in critical categories because you waited too long.

[Image: a linear booking timeline — venue at the far left, favor vendor at the far right, with all other vendors plotted in between]

### The Master Booking Sequence

| Priority | Vendor                           | Book By          | Why                                                         |
| -------- | -------------------------------- | ---------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1        | Venue                            | 12–18 months out | Locks your date. Everything else depends on it.             |
| 2        | Photographer                     | 12–15 months out | In-demand photographers book 12–18 months ahead             |
| 3        | Videographer                     | 12–15 months out | Books on same timeline as photographer                      |
| 4        | Caterer (if separate from venue) | 9–12 months out  | Quality caterers fill their calendar fast                   |
| 5        | DJ or Band                       | 9–12 months out  | Bands especially — good ones book 12+ months out            |
| 6        | Planner / Coordinator            | 9–12 months out  | If you haven't already                                      |
| 7        | Florist                          | 8–10 months out  | Popular florists book up for peak season                    |
| 8        | Hair & Makeup                    | 8–10 months out  | Bridal artists are booked on same schedule as photographers |
| 9        | Officiant                        | 6–9 months out   | More flexibility, but don't leave it to 3 months out        |
| 10       | Transportation                   | 6 months out     | Specialty vehicles book fast for peak dates                 |
| 11       | Cake / Desserts                  | 6 months out     | Some bakers cap their cake schedule                         |
| 12       | Stationery                       | 6–8 months out   | Production time is 4–8 weeks minimum                        |
| 13       | Rentals (linens, furniture)      | 4–6 months out   | Custom rentals need longer lead time                        |
| 14       | Favors                           | 3–4 months out   | If custom or bulk-ordered                                   |
| 15       | Photobooth / extras              | 3–4 months out   | Last priority — fun, not critical                           |

### The "Ask Before You Meet" Rule

Before scheduling any vendor meeting, ask these three questions by email:

1. "Are you available on [date]?"
2. "What is your approximate starting price for [service]?"
3. "Can you briefly describe what's included in your base package?"

This eliminates 40–60% of vendor meetings before they happen — and saves hours of your life.

### The Booking Checklist

- [ ] Venue booked — date locked
- [ ] Photographer booked — date locked
- [ ] Caterer confirmed (in-house or external)
- [ ] Music confirmed (DJ or band)
- [ ] Florist booked
- [ ] Hair & makeup booked
- [ ] Officiant confirmed
- [ ] All contracts signed with deposit paid
- [ ] Master vendor contact sheet created and shared

---

> **Booking Rule #1:** The cost of missing a vendor is always higher than the cost of booking early. Great vendors do not wait.

## 3.2 Photography: The Hire That Matters Most

Of all the vendors you will hire, your photographer is the one whose work you will live with longest. The flowers die. The food is eaten. The photographer's images are with you for 50 years.

[Image: a side-by-side comparison — two wedding ceremony photos, same scene, dramatically different quality — one warm and editorial, one flat and amateur]

### How the Wedding Photography Market Actually Works in 2026

The photography market has three tiers:

**Tier 1: Emerging ($1,500–$2,800)**
Newer photographers building their portfolio. Strong Instagram presence but limited experience with complex lighting, large groups, or timeline pressure. The risk is highest here — but so is the potential for value if you review their work carefully.

**Tier 2: Established ($3,000–$5,500)**
2–8 years of experience, consistent quality, multiple full wedding galleries available to review. This is the sweet spot for most couples. Quality is reliable, and they are still competing for business.

**Tier 3: Premium ($5,500–$12,000+)**
Published work, strong demand, often fully booked 18+ months in advance. Justifiable if photography is your non-negotiable #1 — genuinely out of reach for many budgets.

### What to Actually Look For in a Portfolio

Most couples look at highlight photos. This is a mistake. Highlights are curated to show the photographer at their peak. You want to see the full range.

When reviewing a portfolio, evaluate:

- [ ] **A full gallery from a single wedding** — not highlights, not "favorites," one complete event
- [ ] **Low-light reception photos** — can they shoot a dark dance floor without everything going grainy or flash-harsh?
- [ ] **Group photos** — are they well-composed? Are people squinting? Is everyone visible?
- [ ] **Getting-ready shots** — this is where most weddings start; do they use the light well?
- [ ] **Candid moments** — do people look natural, or do they look posed/uncomfortable?
- [ ] **Consistency across seasons/venues** — not just barn weddings if yours is a hotel ballroom

### The Photography Interview Questions

Ask these at every consultation:

- [ ] "Will you be the photographer at my wedding, or could it be an associate or second shooter in the lead role?"
- [ ] "How many weddings have you shot at a similar venue/lighting situation as mine?"
- [ ] "Walk me through your typical timeline from ceremony start to end of reception — how do you pace the day?"
- [ ] "When do you deliver the final gallery, and in what format?"
- [ ] "How do you handle family formal photos — do you use a shot list?"
- [ ] "What happens if you are incapacitated on my wedding day?"
- [ ] "Do you retain the rights to our photos?" (Most photographers do — understand what you can and cannot do with the images)

### The Photography Contract Must-Haves

- [ ] Photographer's legal name (not just business name) and direct contact information
- [ ] Specific hours of coverage and exact start/end times
- [ ] Number of edited images delivered (range is fine — "approximately 500–700")
- [ ] Turnaround time for delivery of final gallery
- [ ] Format of delivery (online gallery, USB, both)
- [ ] Backup plan if primary photographer cannot attend
- [ ] Rights and usage: what can you print? What can they publish?
- [ ] Second shooter included or at additional cost

### Photography Red Flags

| Red Flag                                        | What It Signals                                          |
| ----------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| No full wedding gallery available to review     | They may not have shot many full weddings                |
| Turnaround time vague ("as soon as possible")   | Lack of professionalism; can become a source of conflict |
| Resistant to sharing backup plan                | No backup plan exists                                    |
| "I'll match any price"                          | Desperation signal — not competition-aware               |
| Contract does not specify who will shoot        | Associate risk — you may not get who you hired           |
| Social media only — no website, no real gallery | Inconsistent work hidden behind curated posts            |

---

> **Booking Rule #2:** View one complete wedding gallery before you commit to any photographer. Every photographer has great highlight shots. Few have consistent complete galleries.

## 3.3 Catering & Bar: Where Budget Goes to Hide

Catering is the most opaque category in wedding planning. Quotes are rarely apples-to-apples. Hidden costs are endemic. And the difference between a $180/head quote and a $260/head quote often comes down to line items that are easy to miss on first read.

[Image: a catering contract annotated with red circles around common hidden fee lines — "service charge," "cake cutting fee," "bar minimum," "gratuity not included"]

### The 6 Hidden Catering Costs

**1. Service Charge**
Most venues and caterers charge 18–22% on top of the per-head food cost. This is NOT the same as gratuity. It goes to the business. Gratuity is separate and expected on top of this.

**2. Cake Cutting Fee**
If your venue provides catering but you bring in an outside cake, expect a $2–$5 per person cutting fee. On 100 guests, that is $200–$500 for the act of slicing cake.

**3. Bar Minimum**
Many venues require you to spend a minimum on bar service. If your crowd is light drinkers, you may pay for alcohol that was never consumed.

**4. Staff Hours**
Setup and breakdown time is sometimes included, sometimes not. "Event time" and "staff time" are different numbers — clarify both.

**5. Rentals (Linens, Glassware, China)**
Venues often quote the food and charge separately for table settings. An itemized contract is essential.

**6. Gratuity**
Standard is 15–20% on top of the food bill for serving staff. Some contracts include this automatically. Some do not. Confirm before your final payment.

### The All-In Per-Head Calculator

Before comparing caterers, calculate the true all-in cost:

> Base per-head rate: $**\_\_**
>
> - Service charge (18–22%): $**\_\_**
> - Rentals per head: $**\_\_**
> - Bar package per head: $**\_\_**
> - Estimated gratuity per head: $**\_\_**
>   = **True all-in per head: $**\_\_****
>   × Guest count: **\_\_**
>   = **True total food & beverage: $**\_\_****

### Catering Interview Questions

- [ ] "Is your quote all-inclusive — does it include service charge, gratuity, rentals, and setup/breakdown?"
- [ ] "Can we see an itemized quote — food, labor, bar, and rentals as separate line items?"
- [ ] "Is a tasting included, and how many people can attend?"
- [ ] "What is your policy for dietary restrictions? How do you handle day-of substitutions?"
- [ ] "What is your staff ratio for a wedding our size?" (Ideal: 1 server per 10–15 guests for plated dinner)
- [ ] "What is your cancellation policy and at what point does the full amount become non-refundable?"
- [ ] "Who is our day-of point of contact?"

### Bar Service Options — Know These Before You Decide

| Bar Type         | What It Includes            | Typical Cost       | Notes                                                |
| ---------------- | --------------------------- | ------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| Full Open Bar    | Beer, wine, spirits, mixers | $65–$95/head       | Most expected option for evening reception           |
| Beer + Wine Only | No liquor                   | $35–$55/head       | 15–20% cost savings; some guests will notice         |
| Consumption Bar  | Guests pay per drink        | $0 upfront         | Guest-unfriendly; avoid unless budget forces it      |
| Dry Wedding      | No alcohol                  | $0                 | Completely acceptable; communicate clearly on invite |
| BYOB             | You supply the alcohol      | $3–$8/head corkage | Significant savings if you do it right               |

**The BYOB Strategy:** If your venue allows it, buying your own alcohol wholesale can save $2,000–$5,000 on a 100-person wedding. You typically need: 1 bottle of wine per 2 guests for 4 hours, 1 case of beer per 25 guests, and hard spirits based on your crowd. Return unopened bottles for maximum efficiency.

### Catering Contract Must-Haves

- [ ] Itemized breakdown (food, bar, rentals, staffing listed separately)
- [ ] Service charge percentage explicitly stated
- [ ] Gratuity: included or not
- [ ] Final headcount deadline and what happens to pricing if count changes
- [ ] Tasting included and date confirmed
- [ ] Named point of contact on the day
- [ ] Overtime rates if the event runs long
- [ ] Cancellation and refund terms

---

> **Booking Rule #3:** Never compare catering quotes without converting them all to the same all-in per-head number. The base rate is almost never the actual rate.

## 3.4 Florals: Maximum Visual Impact, Manageable Cost

Florals are the category most couples overspend on — not because they intended to, but because the choices feel small in isolation and large on the invoice.

[Image: a floral budget breakdown — "what it looks like" vs. "what it costs" showing how quickly $3,500 gets allocated across bouquets, ceremony arch, centerpieces]

### The Floral Budget Reality Check

A typical $3,200 floral budget for a 100-person wedding looks like:

| Item                             | Quantity | Cost Each   | Subtotal    |
| -------------------------------- | -------- | ----------- | ----------- |
| Bridal bouquet                   | 1        | $250–$400   | $300        |
| Bridesmaid bouquets              | 4        | $80–$120    | $400        |
| Boutonnieres                     | 6        | $25–$45     | $210        |
| Ceremony arch / backdrop         | 1        | $600–$1,200 | $900        |
| Ceremony aisle flowers           | 8        | $50–$80     | $500        |
| Cocktail hour arrangements       | 4        | $75–$150    | $500        |
| Reception centerpieces           | 10       | $150–$250   | $2,000      |
| Head table arrangement           | 1        | $300–$600   | $400        |
| **Florist setup + delivery fee** |          |             | $300–$600   |
| **Total**                        |          |             | **~$5,510** |

The gap between $3,200 and $5,510 is why couples go over. Each item feels reasonable. The total does not.

### The 5 Ways to Cut Floral Costs Without Sacrificing the Look

**1. Invest in the ceremony arch, cut elsewhere**
The arch is in every photo. The cocktail hour arrangements are background. Allocate accordingly.

**2. Use greenery-forward designs**
Eucalyptus, ferns, and Italian ruscus cost a fraction of blooms. Lush greenery-filled arrangements can look more expensive than sparse floral ones.

**3. Reduce centerpiece height instead of quantity**
Low arrangements use less material than tall ones. They also enable more cross-table conversation — a guest experience improvement, not just a cost cut.

**4. Buy flowers in season**
Out-of-season flowers are imported and expensive. In-season flowers are abundant and cheap. Your florist knows this — just ask "what is in season for my date?"

**5. Repurpose ceremony arrangements**
Design ceremony florals that can be moved to the reception. Your florist can execute this — but you must specify it in the brief.

### The Florist Interview

- [ ] "Can you walk me through what you'd recommend for our budget and guest count?"
- [ ] "What flowers are in season on our date?"
- [ ] "Do you have photos of weddings at our specific venue?" (They may know the space)
- [ ] "Is setup and delivery included in your quote?"
- [ ] "What does the design process look like — how many revisions are included?"
- [ ] "Can we see your work at a similar budget point?" (Not just their $10K weddings)

### Floral Brief Template

Give this to every florist you consult with:

> **Wedding Date:** **\_\_**
> **Venue:** **\_\_**
> **Color palette:** **\_\_**
> **Vibe/feel words:** **\_\_** (from your Vision Alignment exercise)
> **Items needed:** Bridal bouquet, [X] bridesmaids, [X] boutonnieres, ceremony arch, [X] centerpieces, head table
> **Budget:** $**\_\_**
> **Flowers we love:** **\_\_**
> **Flowers we dislike/cannot use:** **\_\_**
> **Budget priority:** Ceremony OR reception? (florist needs to know)

---

> **Booking Rule #4:** Give every florist the same written brief. Comparing quotes across different assumptions is comparing apples to furniture.

## 3.5 Music: DJ vs. Band — and What You're Actually Buying

The decision between a DJ and a live band is not just about sound preference. It is a decision with cascading effects on your budget, timeline, and guest experience.

[Image: a side-by-side setup diagram — DJ booth on one side, 6-piece band stage layout on the other, with approximate square footage and setup time noted]

### The True Comparison

| Factor            | DJ                       | Live Band                     |
| ----------------- | ------------------------ | ----------------------------- |
| Average cost      | $1,200–$3,000            | $3,500–$10,000+               |
| Setup time        | 1–2 hours                | 2–4 hours                     |
| Space required    | Small (6'×6' minimum)    | Large (20'×20' for 5-piece)   |
| Song catalog      | Virtually unlimited      | Limited to their repertoire   |
| Energy/atmosphere | High control, consistent | Dynamic, electric — when good |
| Ceremony music    | Often included           | Extra charge (if available)   |
| Cocktail hour     | Included                 | Separate charge               |
| Breaks            | None — continuous        | 15 min every 45–60 min        |

**The truth about bands:** A great live band will outperform a great DJ for pure dance-floor energy. A mediocre live band will underperform a mediocre DJ because at least the DJ's music is the original recording. The quality delta is enormous. If you cannot afford a genuinely excellent band ($5,000+), a great DJ will serve you better.

### DJ Interview Questions

- [ ] "How do you handle the transition between cocktail hour, dinner, and dancing?"
- [ ] "Do you bring backup equipment?"
- [ ] "Can you provide MC services, and will you coordinate with our other vendors?"
- [ ] "Can we provide a song list and a Do Not Play list?"
- [ ] "Will you meet us for a planning call 4–6 weeks before the wedding?"
- [ ] "Are you the actual DJ, or could you send someone else?"

### Band Interview Questions

- [ ] "Can I see video of you performing at a wedding — not a showcase, a real wedding?"
- [ ] "What is your repertoire, and can I request specific songs outside of it?"
- [ ] "How many breaks do you take, and what happens to music during breaks?"
- [ ] "What is your setup time requirement, and what power/space do you need?"
- [ ] "Do you provide your own PA system?"
- [ ] "Is the quoted price for the number of musicians I'll actually see on the day?"

### The Do Not Play List

Every couple should create one. Distribute it to your DJ/band AND to your MC. Common inclusions:

- [ ] Ex-related songs (you know which ones)
- [ ] Songs that will trigger specific family members
- [ ] Overplayed wedding tracks you're tired of
- [ ] Songs with inappropriate lyrics for mixed-age crowd
- [ ] Genres that don't match your crowd

### Ceremony Music Options

| Option                         | Cost           | Notes                                   |
| ------------------------------ | -------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| DJ handles ceremony too        | Often included | Efficient but less personal             |
| String quartet                 | $800–$1,800    | Elegant; great for traditional ceremony |
| Acoustic guitarist             | $400–$800      | Versatile and warm                      |
| Vocalist + pianist             | $600–$1,200    | Impactful for processional              |
| Recorded music (Spotify, etc.) | $0             | Works fine; assign a dedicated person   |
| Friend/family musician         | Variable       | Factor in practice time and reliability |

---

> **Booking Rule #5:** Ask every DJ and band candidate: "Can I attend an event where you are performing?" If they say no, the vetting process is incomplete.

## 3.6 Hair & Makeup: The Logistics Most Couples Underestimate

Hair and makeup is not just a beauty decision. It is a logistics decision that affects your wedding morning timeline, your venue load-in schedule, and every person in your wedding party.

[Image: a morning-of timeline chart showing 6 people getting hair and makeup in a 4-hour window, with staggered start times]

### The Math Nobody Does in Advance

Average time per person:

- Bride: 90–120 minutes for hair + makeup
- Bridesmaid: 45–60 minutes for hair, 30–45 for makeup

For a 5-person bridal party (bride + 4 bridesmaids) starting at 8am with a 1pm ceremony:

| Time    | Who          | What                |
| ------- | ------------ | ------------------- |
| 8:00am  | Bridesmaid 1 | Hair                |
| 8:45am  | Bridesmaid 2 | Hair                |
| 9:15am  | Bridesmaid 3 | Hair                |
| 9:45am  | Bridesmaid 4 | Hair                |
| 10:15am | Bride        | Hair begins         |
| 8:00am  | Bridesmaid 4 | Makeup (concurrent) |
| 8:45am  | Bridesmaid 3 | Makeup              |
| 9:30am  | Bridesmaid 2 | Makeup              |
| 10:15am | Bridesmaid 1 | Makeup              |
| 11:15am | Bride        | Makeup begins       |
| 12:15pm | All done     | Buffer time         |

**This requires 2 artists** — one for hair, one for makeup — running concurrently. With one artist, this timeline is impossible without starting at 6am.

### The Hair & Makeup Interview

- [ ] "How many artists will be working on our party on the day?"
- [ ] "What is your timeline for a bridal party of [size]?"
- [ ] "Is the trial included in your package, or is it an additional fee?"
- [ ] "Do you travel to the venue, or do we come to you?"
- [ ] "Is there a travel fee, and what is it based on?"
- [ ] "What products do you use? (Relevant for skin sensitivities, long-wear concerns)"
- [ ] "Do you do a touchup after the ceremony?" (Some artists offer this)

### The Trial Appointment

Every bride should do a trial. Full stop.

- [ ] Schedule the trial 2–4 months before the wedding (far enough to adjust, close enough to be accurate)
- [ ] Do the trial on a day when you have plans — wear the look into the world and see how it holds
- [ ] Bring the same hairpiece, veil, or accessories you'll wear on the day
- [ ] Take photos in natural light AND indoor lighting
- [ ] Rate: longevity, how it photographs, how it feels after 4 hours

### Bridesmaid Beauty Policy

Decide this early and communicate it clearly:

- [ ] Are you paying for bridesmaid hair/makeup? (If so, include in your budget)
- [ ] Are they expected to get it done by your artist, or can they do their own?
- [ ] What is your requirement for day-of look consistency?

---

> **Booking Rule #6:** Book hair and makeup before you finalize your morning timeline. Many couples discover they need an earlier start time — and a second artist — only after they try to build the schedule.

## 3.7 Officiant: The Person Who Actually Marries You

The officiant is one of the most overlooked vendor hires — and one of the most consequential. They are the voice of your ceremony. They set the tone, control the pace, and deliver the words that will live in your memory and your photos.

[Image: an officiant speaking at an outdoor ceremony, couple facing each other, guests visible in background — warm, intimate framing]

### The Officiant Landscape

**Religious officiant (priest, rabbi, minister, imam)**
Most appropriate if ceremony will be held in a house of worship and/or religious tradition is meaningful to both partners. Often requires pre-marital counseling or multiple meetings. May have restrictions on ceremony content.

**Non-denominational / interfaith officiant**
Professional officiants who customize the ceremony to the couple's vision. No religious requirement. Full flexibility on content, length, and structure. Most common choice for non-religious weddings.

**Friend or family member (ordained online)**
Deeply personal and often beautiful. Logistical requirements: most states require ordination through a recognized organization (American Marriage Ministries, Universal Life Church, etc.), and some counties have additional requirements. Have them verify legal filing requirements for your county before agreeing.

**Civil officiant (judge, justice of the peace)**
Most minimal ceremony option. Best for elopements or couples who want a legal ceremony and will celebrate separately.

### The Officiant Interview

- [ ] "Have you officiated at our venue before?"
- [ ] "What is your process for learning about us and customizing the ceremony?"
- [ ] "How long is a typical ceremony you write? (15–20 minutes is standard; 30 is long)"
- [ ] "Will you rehearse with us the night before?"
- [ ] "What do you wear?" (Black robe vs. business formal vs. their style)
- [ ] "Do you use notes or have you memorized the ceremony?"
- [ ] "What is your contingency if you're unable to attend?"

### Ceremony Structure Template

Give this to your officiant as a starting framework:

| Element                 | Length                     | Notes                          |
| ----------------------- | -------------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| Processional            | Until everyone is in place | Set music cue                  |
| Welcome + opening words | 2–3 minutes                | Set the tone                   |
| Readings (optional)     | 2–3 minutes each           | Assign to guests               |
| Address / reflection    | 5–7 minutes                | This is the officiant's speech |
| Exchange of vows        | 3–5 minutes                | Written or repeat-after-me     |
| Ring exchange           | 1–2 minutes                | Cue ring bearer                |
| Pronouncement           | 30 seconds                 | The legal declaration          |
| First kiss              | 30 seconds                 | Cue music                      |
| Recessional             | Until party exits          | Set music cue                  |

### Legal Requirements — Checklist by State

Every state is different. Before your wedding:

- [ ] Research your state's marriage license requirements (blood test? waiting period? residency?)
- [ ] Confirm your county's filing requirements for the signed license
- [ ] Confirm your officiant's standing to legally marry you in your state
- [ ] Know who is responsible for filing the signed license after the ceremony (usually the officiant)

---

> **Booking Rule #7:** Meet your officiant — in person or video — before hiring. A ceremony is the only part of your wedding that cannot be fixed after the fact if it goes wrong.

## 3.8 The Other Vendors — Transportation, Stationery, Cake

These three categories don't need the same depth as the primary vendors — but they have specific traps worth knowing.

[Image: three small vignettes side by side — a vintage getaway car, a beautifully designed invitation suite, and a tiered wedding cake]

### Transportation

**Booking window:** 4–6 months out for specialty vehicles (vintage car, trolley, party bus). 2–3 months for standard limo/car service.

**What to sort out:**

- [ ] How many trips are needed? (Getting wedding party to venue + getting couple away from reception = minimum 2)
- [ ] Who is being transported? (Bride only? Entire wedding party? Guest shuttle?)
- [ ] What is the arrival and departure time on the day?
- [ ] Is gratuity for the driver included or expected separately?
- [ ] What is the overtime rate if the reception runs long?

**Hidden cost:** Most transportation companies charge in hourly minimums (4 hours minimum is common). A 30-minute ride in a $350/hour vehicle = $1,400 minimum. Clarify before booking.

### Stationery

**Booking window:** 6–8 months out for custom design. 4–6 months for semi-custom (pre-designed templates personalized with your details).

**The suite breakdown:**

- Save-the-dates (physical or digital — digital saves $200–$500)
- Invitation suite: invitation, details card, RSVP card, return envelope, outer envelope
- Day-of paper: programs, menus, place cards, table numbers, signage
- Thank-you cards (order at time of invitation printing)

**What most couples forget:**

- [ ] Postage — invitation suites are often heavier than a standard stamp; take a stuffed envelope to the post office and weigh it
- [ ] RSVP envelopes need stamps too — include them or guests won't return them
- [ ] Inner envelopes are traditional but not mandatory; skipping them saves cost

### Wedding Cake & Desserts

**Booking window:** 4–6 months out (reputable bakers cap their wedding schedule).

**The tasting:** Every serious baker offers a tasting. Never book without one. The cake has to taste as good as it looks.

**Per-slice cost:** In 2026, expect $7–$14 per slice for a custom designed cake from a quality baker. A 100-person cake runs $700–$1,400 before delivery and setup.

**Cutting it:** If your venue charges a cake cutting fee, factor it in. Alternatively, a dessert table with pre-cut and plated desserts eliminates the cutting fee entirely.

**The practical alternative:** A small display cake (2-tier) for the cake cutting tradition, supplemented with sheet cakes from the kitchen for serving, can cut cake costs by 50–60% without any guest ever knowing.

---

> **Booking Rule #8:** For every vendor in this section, confirm in writing: exact arrival time, service provided, final payment amount, and what happens if they are late or absent.

## 3.9 The Master Contract Review Checklist

Every vendor contract you sign should be reviewed against this checklist before you commit. Missing items are negotiating opportunities, not deal-breakers — but they must be resolved before signing.

[Image: an annotated contract page with key sections highlighted and checklist items called out in the margin]

### Universal Contract Checklist (Every Vendor)

**Identity & Contact**

- [ ] Full legal name of vendor and business entity (not just business name)
- [ ] Address and primary phone number — not just email
- [ ] Name of the specific person who will be at your wedding
- [ ] Emergency contact method if primary contact is unavailable day-of

**Event Details**

- [ ] Correct wedding date (double-check — errors happen)
- [ ] Correct venue address
- [ ] Exact service start and end time
- [ ] Setup and breakdown time (if applicable)

**Services Defined**

- [ ] Complete itemized list of exactly what is included
- [ ] What is explicitly NOT included (upgrades, extras, travel, etc.)
- [ ] Specific equipment or materials to be provided
- [ ] Staffing level committed (for caterers, beauty, transportation)

**Financials**

- [ ] Total contract value
- [ ] Deposit amount and what it is (refundable vs. non-refundable)
- [ ] Payment schedule: remaining balance due dates
- [ ] What triggers each payment
- [ ] What forms of payment are accepted
- [ ] Overtime/extension rate if the event runs long
- [ ] What happens to pricing if guest count changes

**Cancellation & Emergencies**

- [ ] Cancellation policy (how much notice, what refund, what forfeiture)
- [ ] Force majeure clause — what constitutes a covered emergency
- [ ] What happens if VENDOR cancels or cannot attend
- [ ] Backup plan explicitly stated in writing
- [ ] What you receive if vendor fails to perform (refund? partial refund? replacement?)

**Final Walkthrough**

- [ ] Date for a final confirmation call or walkthrough (4–6 weeks before)
- [ ] Who your day-of contact is (by name)
- [ ] How to reach that person on the day

### Red Flags That Require Resolution Before Signing

| Red Flag                                                      | Action Required                                    |
| ------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| No specific backup plan stated                                | Require written backup plan before signing         |
| "Deposit non-refundable under all circumstances"              | Negotiate force majeure exception                  |
| Contract does not name the specific person performing service | Add their name or walk away                        |
| No cancellation policy for the vendor's cancellation          | Add a clause — what do you receive if they cancel? |
| Final payment due far in advance (more than 60 days before)   | Negotiate to 2–4 weeks before                      |
| Liability disclaimer that waives all vendor responsibility    | Consult a lawyer if the dollar amount warrants it  |
| No overtime rate specified                                    | Add one — or confirm event end time is ironclad    |

### The Signed Contract File

Create a single folder (digital or physical) containing:

- [ ] Signed copy of every vendor contract
- [ ] Proof of every deposit paid (receipt or bank confirmation)
- [ ] Each vendor's emergency contact
- [ ] Balance due dates for each contract (calendar these immediately)
- [ ] Insurance certificates where required by your venue

---

> **Booking Rule #9:** A verbal assurance is worth nothing. If it is not in the contract, it does not exist. Ask for it in writing — every time, without exception.
