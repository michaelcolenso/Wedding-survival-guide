# Section 7: When Things Go Wrong
## The Real-World Problem/Solution Index

Nobody puts "vendor no-show" on their vision board. But after hundreds of weddings, one thing is certain: something will go sideways. It might be small — the wrong ribbon color on the centerpieces. It might be significant — your photographer calls in sick at 6 a.m. Either way, the couples who stay calm and get through it are the ones who had a plan before they needed it.

This section is your crisis reference guide. Dog-ear it. Screenshot it. Send it to your day-of coordinator. Every scenario is structured the same way: what happened, why it happens, what you do right now, and how you prevent it next time. Read it before the wedding so that if you end up using it, you're not reading it for the first time in a panic.

## 7.1 Vendor Problems

### Vendor No-Show on the Day

**Why it happens:** Miscommunication about the date, a double-booking error, personal emergency, or — in rare cases — a vendor who was never reliable to begin with and took your deposit anyway.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] Call the vendor's primary number immediately. Give them three minutes to answer before moving on.
- [ ] Text at the same time as calling. Some people respond to texts faster.
- [ ] Call their emergency or secondary number from the contract.
- [ ] Pull up your vendor contract and confirm the address, date, and time that was agreed upon — occasionally there is a date error that both parties missed.
- [ ] If no response after 10 minutes, assign someone (planner, MOH, parent) to keep trying while you stop watching the phone.
- [ ] Contact a backup vendor immediately. Your planner or venue coordinator should have referrals. Many vendors will show for a cash day-of rate if there is a same-day opening.
- [ ] Document every call attempt with timestamps. You will need this for a dispute.

**Prevention:** Confirm all vendors in writing 2 weeks out and again 48 hours before. Get an emergency cell number at booking. Use vendors with reviews, not just referrals from a single friend.

---

### Vendor Arrives Late — Ceremony Delay Risk

**Why it happens:** Traffic, a previous event running long, poor time management, or failing to account for setup time.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] Call immediately and get an ETA. Get a specific time, not "soon."
- [ ] Assess whether the timeline can absorb the delay. If your ceremony is flexible by 15 minutes, that may be fine. If guests are already seated and officiant has another event, it is not.
- [ ] If it is the officiant who is late: have your planner or a family contact quietly let guests know there will be a short delay. Do not let guests sit in silence and anxiety for 20 minutes without acknowledgment.
- [ ] If it is the caterer who is late: push cocktail hour, add passed appetizers if available, have bartenders open service early.
- [ ] Adjust the rest of the timeline accordingly and communicate changes to your emcee and planner.

**Prevention:** Build 30-minute buffers into your timeline at setup, cocktail hour, and dinner. Vendors who are hard to reach during the planning process are often hard to reach on the day.

---

### Vendor Delivers the Wrong Product

**Why it happens:** Order errors, miscommunication between their office and the delivery team, or a substitution made without notice.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] Do not lose time arguing on-site. Photograph everything immediately.
- [ ] Compare what arrived to your signed contract. If it is wrong, state clearly: "This is not what our contract specifies. Can you correct it before the ceremony?"
- [ ] If correction is impossible (the cake flavor is wrong, the flowers are the wrong color), decide quickly: accept with documentation for a dispute later, or problem-solve a workaround with your planner.
- [ ] For florals: wrong color can sometimes be corrected with ribbon, greenery, or rearranging. Wrong species usually cannot.
- [ ] Document everything in writing via text to the vendor on-site, so you have a timestamped record.

**Prevention:** Review contract details obsessively before signing. Confirm specifics — color names, flavor names, species names — 2 weeks out. If a vendor substitutes without notice, that is a contract breach.

---

### Vendor Bills You for Items Not in the Contract

**Why it happens:** Scope creep, informal verbal agreements that the vendor counted but you did not, or outright predatory billing.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] Do not pay anything you dispute until you have reviewed it against your contract.
- [ ] Request an itemized invoice in writing.
- [ ] Respond in writing: "Item X is not specified in our contract dated [date]. Please send documentation showing where this was agreed upon."
- [ ] If amounts are in dispute and a vendor is threatening to withhold services or deliverables (like your photos), consult your state's small claims process or a consumer protection agency before paying.

**Prevention:** Never agree to add-ons verbally. Every change gets a written addendum signed by both parties.

---

### Vendor Unresponsive After the Wedding

**Why it happens:** Photo delivery delays are the most common version. Vendors sometimes overbook their post-production pipeline, face personal emergencies, or — in the worst case — go out of business.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] Check your contract for the stated delivery timeline. If the deadline has passed, that is a breach.
- [ ] Send a written request via email and text with a specific deadline: "Per our contract, photos were due by [date]. Please confirm delivery by [date + 5 days] or advise on the delay."
- [ ] If no response: file a complaint with your credit card company if the service was paid that way. Dispute the charge.
- [ ] For deposit refund disputes: send a formal demand letter via email with return receipt. If under $10,000, small claims court is often the fastest resolution.

**Prevention:** Pay vendors with a credit card where possible. Choose vendors whose contracts specify delivery deadlines with penalties for non-delivery.

---

### Photographer's Memory Card Fails / Photos Are Corrupted

**Why it happens:** Card failure, accidental deletion, file corruption during transfer, or equipment malfunction. This is rare with professional photographers but it does happen.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] Confirm whether the photographer was using dual-card cameras (most professionals do — files are written to two cards simultaneously). If yes, the backup card likely has the files.
- [ ] Do not format or write new data to any card — this makes recovery harder or impossible.
- [ ] Professional data recovery services can often recover files from corrupted cards. This is the photographer's responsibility to arrange and fund.
- [ ] Document the situation in writing immediately.

**Prevention:** Ask every photographer before booking: "Do you shoot with dual-card cameras?" If the answer is no, or they do not know what that means, keep looking.

## 7.2 Weather and Venue Emergencies

### Rain During Outdoor Ceremony

**Why it happens:** Weather is weather.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] If you have a tent: it goes up. That is why you rented it.
- [ ] If you do not have a tent: move inside. Work with your venue coordinator to identify indoor spaces — a ballroom, foyer, or even a covered parking structure can work in a pinch.
- [ ] Have your planner communicate the change to guests and vendors by text, PA system, or word-of-mouth before they arrive.
- [ ] Adjust the timeline to account for the move.
- [ ] Designate someone to hold umbrellas for the processional if you decide to proceed outdoors.

**Prevention:** Build a rain plan into your venue contract. Ask specifically: "What is the backup space, and is it included at no additional cost?" Get it in writing.

---

### Extreme Heat

**Why it happens:** Outdoor summer ceremonies in full sun with no shade, or inadequately air-conditioned venues.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] Hydrate the wedding party immediately. Keep cold water accessible backstage at all times.
- [ ] If guests are seated in direct sun: have your coordinator offer shade solutions (fans, umbrellas, moving the ceremony start time).
- [ ] Move floral arrangements into shade or air conditioning as early as possible. Heat wilts flowers fast.
- [ ] If anyone shows signs of heat exhaustion (dizziness, pale skin, heavy sweating), move them indoors and call 911 if symptoms are severe.

**Prevention:** Outdoor summer ceremonies before 5 p.m. in hot climates require heat mitigation planning. Budget for fans, water stations, and parasols.

---

### Power Outage at the Venue

**Why it happens:** Grid failure, blown breakers from equipment overload, or severe weather.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] Contact venue management immediately — this is their problem to solve.
- [ ] Check whether your DJ or band has battery-powered equipment or can run on a generator.
- [ ] Most phones have flashlight apps. Candles, if already part of your decor, can provide meaningful light.
- [ ] If the kitchen loses power: food safety becomes a concern. Your caterer must assess whether service can continue.
- [ ] Ask the venue whether they have a generator backup. Many event venues do.

**Prevention:** Ask venues: "Do you have a backup generator? Has there been a power issue in the last 12 months?" Especially important for older buildings.

---

### Venue Overbooking or Previous Event Running Over

**Why it happens:** Poor venue management. The event before yours is still in teardown when you need to begin setup.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] Your planner should be on-site for setup and escalate immediately to the venue manager.
- [ ] Document the setup start delay with timestamps and photos.
- [ ] Prioritize what must be set up for ceremony to begin on time — flowers and decor can catch up, but you need chairs, audio, and the officiant in place.
- [ ] Flag this formally to the venue in writing after the wedding. Depending on your contract, you may be owed a partial refund.

**Prevention:** Your venue contract should specify your setup window with a start time guaranteed in writing. Ask about same-day events before booking.

---

### Noise Complaint or Forced Music Shutdown

**Why it happens:** Local ordinances, neighbor complaints, or venue license restrictions.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] Do not argue with venue staff or local authorities on the night. That battle is for the next day, in writing.
- [ ] Ask for the specific ordinance or rule being cited. Get the name of the person making the call.
- [ ] Shift to acoustic music, a playlist at lower volume, or transition to quieter parts of the evening.
- [ ] Document the time, the reason given, and who made the call.

**Prevention:** Ask venues before booking: "What are your music cutoff rules? Is there a decibel limit? Have you had noise complaints in the last year?" Get the cutoff time in writing.

## 7.3 Family and Guest Situations

### Parent or Family Member Makes a Scene

**Why it happens:** Alcohol, old wounds, remarriage tensions, or unmet expectations about the day.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] This is not the couple's job to manage on their wedding day. Designate a family point person — a trusted sibling, family friend, or planner — before the wedding to handle exactly this.
- [ ] The response is calm, firm, and private: "Let's step outside for a minute." Never address a scene publicly.
- [ ] Script for the point person: "I can see you're upset. I want to make sure [couple's names] have a beautiful day. Can we talk about this after the reception?"
- [ ] If the scene escalates or becomes threatening: involve venue security or call 911 without hesitation.
- [ ] Do not let the couple engage with the conflict during the reception. Protect their experience.

**Prevention:** Have an honest pre-wedding conversation with high-risk family members. Assign a handler in advance, not the day of.

---

### Uninvited Guest Shows Up

**Why it happens:** A guest brought a plus-one who was not invited, or someone saw the invitation and assumed they were included.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] Have a greeter or coordinator manage the guest list at the door — not the couple.
- [ ] Script: "I don't see your name on our list, but let me check with the couple's coordinator. Can you give me just a moment?"
- [ ] If there is genuinely no room: be honest and kind. "We have a seated dinner with assigned seating and we aren't able to accommodate additional guests tonight. I'm so sorry."
- [ ] If there is room and the couple approves: add them quietly and have catering adjust.

**Prevention:** Assign a guest list manager for the door. Make sure your invitation language is clear about whether plus-ones are included.

---

### Guest Has Too Much to Drink

**Why it happens:** Open bars, long cocktail hours, nervous guests, or people who started drinking before arriving.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] Alert your bartender quietly. A good bartender will slow service or switch to mocktails without confrontation.
- [ ] Assign someone to discreetly sit with the guest, get food in them, and get them water.
- [ ] If they are becoming disruptive: escort them outside with a helper and arrange a safe ride. Call a rideshare, call their emergency contact, or contact a family member.
- [ ] Do not let them drive. This is non-negotiable.
- [ ] Script for the escort: "Hey, let's get some air and grab some food — I've been wanting to catch up with you."

**Prevention:** Brief your bartender before the reception. Most professional bartenders know how to manage this without being told, but it helps to confirm.

### Relationship Conflict Erupts at the Reception

**Why it happens:** Divorced parents seated near each other, estranged family members, or unresolved tension that shows up when alcohol is involved.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] Separate the parties. Physical distance is the first tool.
- [ ] Assign a point person to each side to keep them occupied and away from each other.
- [ ] Use the seating chart as your first line of defense — this should have been managed in advance.
- [ ] If a confrontation is happening publicly: the designated family handler steps in. Same script as above — private, calm, firm.

**Prevention:** Build the seating chart with conflict awareness, not just family proximity. If two people cannot be in the same room without conflict, they should not have a clear sightline to each other.

---

### Guest Has a Medical Emergency

**Why it happens:** Heat, alcohol, existing health conditions, or accidents.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] Call 911 immediately if the person is unconscious, unresponsive, having difficulty breathing, or showing signs of a cardiac event.
- [ ] Do not leave the person alone. Assign someone to stay with them until help arrives.
- [ ] Ask if anyone in the room is a medical professional. In a room of 100+ people, there is often a nurse, EMT, or doctor present.
- [ ] Have a venue staff member meet the paramedics at the entrance to guide them in.
- [ ] Keep the couple informed but do not require them to leave the event unless they choose to.

**Prevention:** Know where the nearest AED is located at your venue. Ask venue staff at the walk-through.

---

### A Key Wedding Party Member Bails Before the Wedding

**Why it happens:** Personal emergencies, relationship conflicts, or — more often than people expect — cold feet about the responsibility.

**What to do right now (if it is weeks out):**
- [ ] Have an honest conversation to understand whether the situation is reversible.
- [ ] If it is not reversible: reassign responsibilities immediately. Who can step into their role? What duties were they handling?
- [ ] If they were holding items (rings, dress, accessories): retrieve them.
- [ ] Notify your vendors — especially the photographer and florist — of the change.
- [ ] Adjust attire orders if possible. Alterations take time.

**What to do right now (if it is days out):**
- [ ] Triage their responsibilities. Not all duties need a replacement person — they just need to be covered.
- [ ] Consider whether you want them present as a guest or prefer they do not attend.

**Prevention:** Have explicit conversations with wedding party members about what is expected. Formal "Will you be my bridesmaid/groomsman" asks that include a written list of responsibilities reduce surprises.

## 7.4 Personal and Couple Emergencies

### One Partner Is Sick on the Wedding Day

**Why it happens:** It is a high-stress event that required months of sleep deprivation to plan. The immune system is not impressed.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] Call your doctor or urgent care first thing if symptoms are significant. Do this before makeup, before getting dressed.
- [ ] Manage the symptoms aggressively: fever reducers, anti-nausea medication, electrolytes, rest until absolutely necessary to move.
- [ ] Communicate your status to your planner so adjustments to the timeline can be made — later start, shorter receiving line, seated ceremony if needed.
- [ ] If the sick partner is genuinely unable to proceed: consult your venue and vendors about postponement. Wedding insurance (if purchased) may cover this.

**Prevention:** Prioritize sleep in the final two weeks. Avoid new foods and restaurant roulette the week before. Stop drinking alcohol 3+ days out.

---

### Wedding Dress Emergency

**Why it happens:** Accidents happen. Bustles break. Seams split during an enthusiastic first dance.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] Your emergency kit should already contain: a needle and thread (white, ivory, and nude), safety pins in multiple sizes, a tide-to-go pen, fashion tape, and a small scissors.
- [ ] For a bustle break: safety pins are almost always sufficient to rebuild a makeshift bustle.
- [ ] For a seam split: safety pins from the inside or a fast hand-stitch can close the gap.
- [ ] For a stain: blot immediately with cold water. Do not rub. Use the stain remover pen. If the fabric is delicate, blotting with a dry cloth first buys time.
- [ ] A torn hem can be safety-pinned from the inside in under two minutes.

**Prevention:** Pack the emergency kit. Practice the bustle before the wedding day so more than one person knows how to redo it.

---

### Running 30+ Minutes Behind on the Timeline

**Why it happens:** Getting-ready time is almost always underestimated. Hair and makeup run long. People are not where they need to be.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] Identify what can be cut or compressed without affecting the ceremony. A first look, extra portraits, or extended cocktail hour are the first places to look.
- [ ] Communicate the delay to your planner so downstream vendors — the caterer, the DJ, the officiant — can adjust.
- [ ] Prioritize ruthlessly. The ceremony starts on time or close to it. Everything else bends.
- [ ] Do not try to run faster by skipping communication. Uncoordinated rushing makes things worse.

**Prevention:** Build 30-minute buffers into every getting-ready phase. Have your planner give you a fake start time that is 20 minutes earlier than real.

---

### Pre-Ceremony Panic Attack or Breakdown

**Why it happens:** This is one of the most significant days of a person's life. The body sometimes responds accordingly.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] Get the person somewhere quiet and private.
- [ ] Use grounding techniques: name five things you can see, four you can touch, three you can hear.
- [ ] Slow breathing: in for four counts, hold for four, out for four.
- [ ] Do not rush them. Give them ten minutes before escalating.
- [ ] Have a trusted person — not the other partner — with them. The couple should not see each other in crisis before the ceremony if avoidable.
- [ ] If anxiety is ongoing and medication was prescribed, ensure they have taken it.

**Prevention:** Build a 30-minute quiet buffer before the ceremony for the wedding party. Reduce the number of people in the getting-ready space. Eat breakfast.

---

### Marriage License Filing Error Discovered After the Wedding

**Why it happens:** Incomplete paperwork, incorrect information, or the officiant failing to file in time.

**What to do right now:**
- [ ] First: confirm the error by contacting the county clerk where you filed.
- [ ] If the license was not filed: your officiant may still be able to file if within the valid window.
- [ ] If the license was filed with incorrect information (wrong spelling, wrong date of birth): contact the clerk's office for an amendment process. This is correctable.
- [ ] If the license was never valid to begin with: you will need to re-marry in a civil ceremony. This is rare and fixable — it does not undo your wedding.
- [ ] Note: your wedding was real. A paperwork error does not change that.

**Prevention:** Confirm your officiant is legally authorized to perform marriages in your state. Confirm they filed within the required window. Ask for a copy of the filed license.

## Day-of Triage Quick Reference

When something goes wrong, the first question is always: who handles this, and does the couple need to know right now?

| Problem | First Call | Second Call | Escalate to Couple? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vendor no-show | Vendor emergency line | Planner/coordinator backup list | Yes, after backup is in motion |
| Vendor late (30+ min) | Vendor directly | Planner to adjust timeline | Only if ceremony is affected |
| Wrong flowers delivered | Floral vendor | Planner | No — handle and document |
| Wrong cake delivered | Bakery | Planner | Only if unserviceable |
| DJ/band equipment failure | Vendor | Venue A/V contact | Yes, if ceremony audio is at risk |
| Power outage | Venue manager | Electrician/generator rental | Yes |
| Rain — no backup plan | Venue coordinator | Planner | Yes |
| Extreme heat | Venue coordinator | Rental company (fans) | Only if wedding party is affected |
| Noise shutdown | Venue manager | Planner | Yes |
| Guest too intoxicated | Bartender | Designated handler | No — handle quietly |
| Uninvited guest | Door coordinator | Planner | Only if guest is aggressive |
| Family conflict | Designated family handler | Planner | No — keep couple out of it |
| Medical emergency | 911 | Venue staff | Yes, briefly |
| Dress emergency | MOH / emergency kit | Seamstress if on call | No |
| Running 30 min behind | Planner | All vendors via text | Yes, briefly |
| Pre-ceremony breakdown | Trusted attendant | Planner | Couple stays separate |
| Wrong venue setup | Venue coordinator | Planner | Only if uncorrectable |
| Officiant late | Officiant directly | Backup officiant if exists | Yes |
| License error (post-wedding) | County clerk | Attorney if needed | Yes |
| Guest medical non-emergency | Venue staff | Designated handler | No |

The rule: the couple should only be pulled in when a decision requires their input that no one else can make. Everything else is what you hired a planner and designated handlers to manage.

---

*The couples who look back on their wedding day without regret — even when things went sideways — are the ones who decided in advance that they were going to handle whatever came up. Not because they were fearless, but because they had a plan. Now you have one.*
