# Section 6: The Reception Playbook

## Timeline, Music, Flow, Toasts, and the Art of a Great Party

[Image: wide-angle shot of a full dance floor at golden hour, string lights overhead, guests laughing and dancing]

---

> **"The ceremony is what you remember. The reception is what everyone else talks about for the next decade."**

You have planned the ceremony with reverence. You have obsessed over the flowers. You have cried at the rehearsal dinner. All of that is real and it matters. But the reception — the four to six hours that follow — is where a wedding becomes a legend or a cautionary tale.

This section is your operating manual for the reception. Not the Pinterest version. The real version, with actual timelines, real scripts, and honest guidance on what goes wrong and how to prevent it.

---

## 6.1 The Reception Philosophy

### The Difference Between Good and Great

A good reception runs on time, the food is warm, and no one cries in the bathroom (unless they want to). A great reception has energy — it rises and falls intentionally, guests feel cared for, and you actually enjoy your own party.

The difference is almost never budget. It is almost always **intentional design**.

Most couples spend months on the visual details of the reception and about two hours on the actual flow. Then they wonder why the middle of the night dragged, why the dance floor was empty at 9 PM, or why they spent most of their reception doing table rounds instead of dancing.

### The Timeline Is the Most Important Document You Will Create

Your reception timeline is not a suggestion. It is a production schedule. Every vendor — your caterer, DJ, photographer, videographer, florist, and coordinator — runs their night off of it. When it breaks, everything breaks with it.

The timeline tells the story of your reception before it happens. A well-built timeline accounts for energy peaks (grand entrance, first dance, cake cutting), energy valleys (dinner service, long toast runs), and recovery moments (a well-timed open dance set after a slow dinner).

You will write your timeline in Section 4. In this section, you will learn what to put in it.

### The 3 Pillars of a Great Reception

| Pillar | What It Means | What Breaks It |
|---|---|---|
| **Energy Management** | The night has a shape — it builds, peaks, and lands intentionally | Toasts during dinner, dead floor at 9 PM, cake too late |
| **Guest Experience** | Every guest feels welcomed, fed, included, and entertained | Long gaps, unclear signage, tables forgotten during rounds |
| **Couple Protection** | You eat, you dance, you are present — not managing logistics | No coordinator, no clear vendor point of contact, no timeline |

> **Reception Rule #1:** The reception exists to celebrate you, not perform for your guests. Design it for the couple you are, not the wedding you think people expect.

## 6.2 The Grand Entrance

[Image: wedding party lined up outside venue doors, DJ booth visible, guests standing and clapping in anticipation]

### Why the Grand Entrance Matters

The grand entrance is the reset button. It signals to every guest that the party has officially started. It brings the room to its feet. It gives you — the couple — a moment to breathe, look at each other, and step into the joy that the next few hours are supposed to be.

A weak entrance creates a weak first impression. Guests sit back down and the energy drops before it ever built. A strong entrance puts the room on its feet and sets the tone for everything that follows.

The entrance is also one of the most photographed and filmed moments of the entire reception. It is worth thirty minutes of intentional planning.

### Order of Introduction Options

**Option A: Wedding Party First, Couple Last (Most Common)**
Each couple or individual in the wedding party is introduced and walks in to music. The energy builds. Guests are on their feet. Then the couple enters to a peak moment in the song. This is the most dramatic and most commonly executed option.

**Option B: Couple Only (Intimate or Small Weddings)**
No wedding party introduction. The couple enters alone, usually to a single song chosen for emotional resonance. Works beautifully for weddings under 75 guests where individual introductions would feel formal.

**Option C: No Formal Entrance**
For very casual receptions or micro-weddings where guests have already mingled during cocktail hour. The couple simply arrives, and the MC welcomes everyone. This can feel anticlimactic — use it only if it genuinely matches your personality and venue.

### Entrance Music Selection Guide

| Energy Level | What It Does | Timing Sweet Spot |
|---|---|---|
| **High Energy (BPM 120+)** | Dance floor ready, crowd engaged | Best for large weddings 100+ guests |
| **Mid Energy (BPM 90–119)** | Celebratory but controlled | Most versatile — works for most receptions |
| **Low/Emotional** | Intimate, meaningful, sometimes tears | Small weddings, micro-weddings, garden settings |

**Key rules for entrance music:**
- [ ] The song should be long enough for the full wedding party introduction
- [ ] Your actual entrance (as a couple) should hit at a recognizable, high-energy moment in the song — the chorus, the drop, the key change
- [ ] Avoid songs that are too slow to walk confidently to
- [ ] Avoid songs with lyrics that will clash awkwardly with the MC's introduction
- [ ] Give your DJ the exact timestamp where you want your couple's entrance to hit

### The Entrance Script Template

This is what your DJ or MC will actually say. Customize and send this to them in writing at least two weeks before the wedding.

---

**ENTRANCE SCRIPT — [COUPLE NAMES] — [DATE]**

*"Ladies and gentlemen, please rise and put your hands together as we introduce the wedding party of [Couple Name 1] and [Couple Name 2]!"*

*[Music begins]*

*"First, please welcome [Name and Name], [relationship — e.g., maid of honor and best man]..."*

*[Continue for all wedding party members or couples]*

*"And now — the moment you have all been waiting for — please welcome for the very first time as a married couple, [Full Name] and [Full Name]!"*

*[Music hits peak moment — couple enters]*

---

Send this script to your MC or DJ verbatim. Ask them to confirm receipt and read it back to you at the rehearsal or in a call before the wedding.

### Common Entrance Mistakes

- [ ] Failing to send the script in advance (your DJ improvises, and the energy dies)
- [ ] Not doing a sound check before guests arrive
- [ ] Forgetting to assign someone to line up the wedding party
- [ ] Choosing a song that fades out before the couple enters
- [ ] Not practicing the actual walk — including pacing, arm positions, and where to stop

> **Reception Rule #2:** The grand entrance is not the time to improvise. Script it, rehearse it, and send every word to your MC in writing.

## 6.3 First Dance, Parent Dances, and Wedding Party Dances

[Image: couple sharing first dance under soft spotlight, guests forming a circle around them, photographer kneeling at the edge]

### The Choreography Decision — An Honest Assessment

You have two options: take lessons, or do not. Here is the honest guide.

**Take lessons if:**
- Either of you feels genuinely self-conscious dancing in front of others
- You want a choreographed moment (a dip, a spin sequence, a surprise turn)
- Your song is longer than 3 minutes and unedited
- You have seen the TikTok video of what a first dance can be and you want that

**Skip lessons if:**
- You already dance together comfortably
- You are comfortable doing a simple sway-and-turn for 2–3 minutes
- Your budget is tight and this is not a priority for you
- The thought of choreography gives either of you anxiety

**The compromise most couples use:** Take one 90-minute lesson, not to choreograph a full routine, but to feel confident in a basic frame, a smooth turn, and a graceful dip if desired. That single lesson eliminates most first-dance awkwardness.

**Regardless of your choice: Edit the song.** Most first-dance songs are 4–5 minutes long. A 4-minute first dance feels like a very long time when everyone is watching you. Edit or ask your DJ to fade to 2.5–3 minutes.

### Song Selection Guide by Dance Type

| Dance | Tone | Practical Length | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **First Dance** | Meaningful, romantic | 2:30 – 3:00 | Edit if necessary; avoid very slow tempos |
| **Parent Dance (Mother/Son)** | Sentimental | 2:30 – 3:00 | Can be combined or sequential |
| **Parent Dance (Father/Daughter)** | Sentimental | 2:30 – 3:00 | One of the highest-emotion moments of the night |
| **Wedding Party Dance** | Upbeat, fun | 1:30 – 2:00 | Optional; usually transitions to open dancing |

### Timing and Placement in the Reception

Do not stack all dances at the beginning of the reception. This is the most common timeline mistake and it kills energy for the next two hours.

**Recommended placement:**

| Time | Event |
|---|---|
| Grand entrance | First dance immediately follows (high energy to intimate) |
| During or just after dinner | Parent dances (more intimate, guests are seated and attentive) |
| After cake cutting | Open dancing begins |

This spacing keeps the night from front-loading all of its emotional moments into the first 30 minutes and leaving nothing for later.

### The "Open It Up" Transition

After the last parent dance, your DJ should not pause. The best transition is immediate: the parent dance ends, the DJ says something like *"Alright everyone, let's get on that dance floor,"* and the first open-dancing song starts before guests have a chance to sit back down. Pre-select this song with your DJ. It should be universally loved, upbeat, and a guaranteed crowd-pleaser — not a deep cut or an obscure favorite.

### Handling Complicated Family Dance Situations

This is where we need to talk honestly.

**If a parent is deceased:** You have options. Many couples choose to skip the parent dance entirely and open the floor. Others do the dance and dedicate it aloud to the parent who is gone. Some couples dance with a living grandparent, an aunt or uncle, or a close family friend who stepped into that role. There is no wrong answer — only what feels true to you.

**If parents are divorced:** You can do two separate dances, or you can do a combined dance where the parent cuts in at the midpoint. Brief your DJ and your MC on exactly what will happen so there is no awkward pause.

**If the relationship is estranged or nonexistent:** You are not obligated to perform a dance that feels false. Simply omit it, and no explanation is required. Ask your MC not to announce it.

**Script for a deceased parent acknowledgment:**

*"Before we open the dance floor, [Name] and [Name] would like to take a moment to honor [Parent Name], who is with us in spirit tonight. This next song is dedicated to [them/her/him]."*

[ ] Send this script to your MC in writing. Do not leave it to improvisation.

> **Reception Rule #3:** No dance is mandatory. Every dance should be performed because it is true — not because tradition requires it.

## 6.4 The Toast System

[Image: best man standing at microphone, champagne glass raised, guests smiling and looking up from tables]

### Who Gives Toasts, In What Order, and For How Long

The standard toast order is: maid of honor, best man, then optionally parents or other chosen speakers. That order works because it moves from personal (the couple's closest friends) to broader (family perspective).

**The maximum toast rule: 3 speakers, 3 minutes each.**

That is nine minutes of toasts total. It feels short when you are planning it. It feels exactly right — or slightly too long — when you are sitting at the head table trying to look engaged while your stomach growls.

Every minute beyond three is a minute where guest energy drops, food gets colder, and the bar gets busier. Toasts are wonderful. Long toasts are an imposition.

### How to Brief Your Toast-Givers

Do not assume your toast-givers know what to do. Most people have never given a wedding toast. Many will be nervous. Some will overprepare. Some will underprepare and free-style in a way that makes you sweat.

Send every toast-giver this briefing at least three weeks before the wedding. (The full template is in Section 6.9.)

**Key points your briefing must cover:**
- [ ] You have a 3-minute maximum. Practice with a timer.
- [ ] Structure: Open with how you know the couple. Share one specific story. Close with a toast.
- [ ] What not to say (see full list in 6.9)
- [ ] Where to stand, where the microphone will be, and who to hand it to afterward
- [ ] Alcohol guidance: one drink before the toast, not three

### Toast Timing in the Reception Timeline

**Do not put all toasts during dinner service.** The most common mistake is scheduling all toasts while plates are being cleared — creating a 15-minute dead zone where guests are half-eating, half-listening, and the energy is at its lowest.

**Recommended toast placement:**

| Option | When | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| **Before dinner is served** | Guests are focused, not distracted by food | High attention, but may feel long if guests are hungry |
| **Between courses** | Natural pause in service | Works well; coordinate with catering |
| **After first course, before entree** | Sweet spot — guests have had a drink | Most recommended option |

Do not do toasts during the main course. Plates clinking, servers moving, guests half-distracted — it does not serve the speakers or you.

### The Microphone Handoff Protocol

Assign one person — your coordinator, your DJ, or a trusted groomsman — to manage the microphone physically. This person:

- [ ] Confirms the mic is live and working before the first speaker
- [ ] Stands near the speaking area
- [ ] Accepts the mic after each speaker and hands it to the next
- [ ] Returns the mic to the DJ booth or podium after toasts end

A microphone left sitting on a table gets tapped, dropped, or accidentally activated at inopportune moments. Do not let this happen.

### Alcohol and Toasts: Risk Management

One drink before a toast: charming. Three drinks: a liability. You cannot control this entirely, but you can reduce the risk.

- [ ] Schedule toasts early in the evening before the bar has been open for two hours
- [ ] Ask your MC to introduce each speaker by name and walk to them — this gives the speaker a clear, visible cue to be ready, and a moment of sobriety
- [ ] If someone's toast goes off the rails (too long, too personal, too loud), pre-assign your coordinator or a trusted friend to signal your DJ to bring up the music — this is a professional and graceful way to end an overstaying toast
- [ ] Brief your DJ: *"If a toast goes over 4 minutes, gently raise the background music as a signal."*

> **Reception Rule #4:** Toasts are a gift. Long toasts are a hostage situation. Brief your speakers, enforce the time limit, and protect the room's energy.

## 6.5 Dinner Service Mastery

[Image: elegantly set reception dinner table from above, name cards at each place, centerpiece in center, multiple plate courses visible]

### Plated vs. Buffet vs. Stations vs. Cocktail-Style: The Final Decision Guide

| Format | Best For | Watch Out For |
|---|---|---|
| **Plated** | Formal weddings, strong coordination with caterer, per-person control | Slower service, need accurate headcount by meal choice |
| **Buffet** | Casual or rustic events, varied dietary needs, larger groups | Lines, cold food at peak times, guests standing too long |
| **Stations** | Interactive, cocktail-party feel, diverse food cultures | Guests may not eat enough without guided rounds, requires space |
| **Cocktail-Style (no sit-down)** | Intimate, social, budget-conscious options | Guests over 60 will need seating; harder to feel "dinner complete" |

### The Dinner Timeline: How Long It Actually Takes

Most couples dramatically underestimate how long dinner takes to execute. Here is the reality for a wedding of 100 guests:

| Service Style | Time to Serve First Course | Time to Clear | Full Dinner Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Plated (3 courses) | 20–25 min | 10 min per course | 75–90 minutes |
| Buffet (100 guests) | 30–45 min (lines) | Ongoing | 60–75 minutes |
| Stations | 45–60 min (people graze) | Ongoing | 60–90 minutes |

Build these times into your timeline honestly. If your caterer says dinner will take 45 minutes for 120 guests on a plated 3-course menu, ask them how they will execute that and push back if the math does not work.

### The Dietary Restriction Management System

Pre-wedding preparation is only half the job. Here is the day-of execution checklist.

- [ ] Print a dietary restriction master list and give it to your head caterer and venue coordinator
- [ ] Use place card notation to mark special meals (a small symbol or colored dot on the card)
- [ ] Pre-place special meals at the appropriate seats before guests sit if possible
- [ ] Assign one catering captain as the dietary restriction point person — one call, one person
- [ ] Confirm with each dietary guest that their meal arrived correctly within 5 minutes of service

### Vendor Meals: This Is Non-Negotiable

Your vendors need to eat. Your photographer, videographer, DJ, coordinator, and any assistants need a real meal — not a vendor tray of leftovers delivered at 9 PM. A photographer who has not eaten since noon is a photographer who misses shots at 8 PM.

- [ ] Confirm vendor meals with your caterer in writing at the final meeting
- [ ] Schedule vendor meal time in your timeline (typically during guest dinner service)
- [ ] Designate a space for vendors to eat — not the main reception room, not the parking lot
- [ ] Confirm the meal matches your caterer's service timing so vendors are available before key events

### The Couple's Dinner Rule

You will be tempted to spend your dinner hour doing table rounds — visiting every table, thanking every guest. You will arrive at your own table to find cold food, an empty champagne flute, and your spouse making a face at you.

**The rule: 20 minutes at the table, phones off, actually eating.** Assign your coordinator or a trusted wedding party member to manage the table round schedule. You visit tables before dinner or after the first course — not during your only meal of the day.

### Bar Service Pacing Strategy

- [ ] Open the bar during cocktail hour, not before the ceremony
- [ ] Serve wine with dinner automatically (poured by servers, not self-serve)
- [ ] Slow service subtly during toasts — guests do not need to be refilling during a two-minute speech
- [ ] Last call 30 minutes before the end of the reception
- [ ] Close the bar fully 15 minutes before reception ends

> **Reception Rule #5:** Feed your vendors, protect your dinner time, and trust that table rounds can happen before the entree arrives — not instead of eating.

## 6.6 The Dance Floor Architecture

[Image: DJ booth setup with professional lighting rig, empty dance floor just before reception kicks off, lighting casting warm pink and gold tones]

### Why Dance Floors Fail

Most empty dance floors are not the result of boring guests. They are the result of one or more of these structural failures:

| Failure | Why It Kills the Floor |
|---|---|
| **Floor too large** | Guests feel exposed dancing in a vast empty space |
| **Wrong placement** | Floor in a corner, far from the bar or dinner area |
| **No DJ briefing** | DJ plays deep cuts no one knows before the floor is warm |
| **Bad timing** | First open-dance set starts too early (guests still eating) or too late (guests are tired) |
| **Stacked slow songs** | Energy drops before it can build |

### The DJ Briefing Document

Send this to your DJ at least two weeks before the wedding. Do not rely on a verbal conversation.

---

**DJ BRIEFING — [COUPLE NAMES] — [DATE] — [VENUE]**

**Grand Entrance Song:** [Title — Artist] — Start at timestamp [X:XX] for couple's entrance

**First Dance:** [Title — Artist] — Edit/fade at [X:XX]

**Mother-Son Dance:** [Title — Artist]

**Father-Daughter Dance:** [Title — Artist]

**First Open Dance Song (crowd-starter):** [Title — Artist]

**Music Style Preference (check all that apply):**
- [ ] Top 40 (current)
- [ ] Classic hits (70s/80s/90s)
- [ ] Hip-hop and R&B
- [ ] Country
- [ ] Latin
- [ ] Rock

**Do Not Play List:** [See Section 6.6 below]

**Crowd Read Permission:** Yes — please read the crowd and adjust if needed

**Key Moments and Timestamps:** [Attach your full reception timeline]

**Point of Contact Night-Of:** [Name — Phone Number]

### The Music Arc: How a Great Reception Builds and Peaks

A great reception has a shape. Think of it like a story arc.

| Phase | Time | Energy | What's Playing |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cocktail Hour** | Hour 1 | Warm, social | Jazz, acoustic, background music |
| **Entrance / First Dances** | First 30 min | High then intimate | Entrance song, first dance, parent dances |
| **Dinner** | 60–90 min | Low-medium | Dinner playlist, soft background |
| **Floor Opens / Builds** | 30 min | Rising | Crowd-starter, familiar hits, rising energy |
| **Peak** | 45–60 min | Maximum | Best floor-fillers of the night |
| **Wind Down** | Final 30 min | Slowing | Slower songs, last dance, send-off |

Brief your DJ on this arc explicitly. A DJ who understands the shape of your night will manage energy far better than one receiving a playlist with no context.

### The Dead Floor Emergency Protocol

Even with great planning, the floor sometimes empties. Here is what to do — in order.

- [ ] **Step 1:** Your coordinator signals the DJ quietly. The DJ transitions to a known crowd-pleaser.
- [ ] **Step 2:** Your wedding party hits the floor immediately. Five people dancing looks like a party. Zero people dancing looks like a failure.
- [ ] **Step 3:** The MC makes a light, energetic invite: *"We need everyone on that dance floor — right now."*
- [ ] **Step 4:** If none of that works within 10 minutes, move up the next scheduled event (cake cutting, anniversary dance) to reset the energy.

Pre-plan this with your coordinator and DJ so no one is panicking in the moment.

### Last Dance and Send-Off Strategy

The last dance should be announced — not just fade in. Your MC should say something like: *"This is your last chance to get on the dance floor. This one's for [Couple Names]."* Then one final song that means something to you.

Do not end with a slow, melancholy song. End with energy and love, then move directly into the send-off.

### The "Do Not Play" List

This matters more than most couples realize. Make your Do Not Play list specific and share it with your DJ in writing.

**Common entries:**
- [ ] Songs that remind you of an ex
- [ ] Songs with lyrics that will clash with your crowd's demographics
- [ ] The "Macarena," "YMCA," or any song you genuinely hate but fear the DJ will play
- [ ] Anything with explicit content if children are present
- [ ] Any song that has a specific painful memory for either of you

Your DJ will not be offended. A professional DJ will be grateful.

> **Reception Rule #6:** A great dance floor is designed, not wished for. Brief your DJ in writing, build the arc intentionally, and have a dead-floor protocol ready before you need it.

## 6.7 Special Moments and Traditions

[Image: couple cutting wedding cake together, photographer capturing the moment from slightly below, guests laughing in background]

### Cake Cutting: When, How, and the Photo Window

**When:** The cake cutting traditionally happens after dinner, before open dancing. This is a clean transition moment — dinner is done, the floor is about to open, and the cake signals a shift in energy.

Do not cut the cake too late (after 9 PM, when guests are in full party mode and no one is paying attention) or too early (during cocktail hour, when it feels anticlimactic).

**How:** Your caterer or coordinator will handle the actual cutting and service. Your job is to show up for the ceremonial first cut, hold the knife together, smile for the photo window, and make a decision about the cake-in-face moment.

**The cake-in-face decision:** Have this conversation before the wedding. If one of you hates the idea and the other finds it funny, that is not a good improvised moment. Decide together, tell the photographer, and execute what you agreed on.

**Photo window:** Tell your photographer when the cake cutting is happening. They need 5 minutes of positioning time. Do not cut the cake without your photographer in position.

### Bouquet and Garter Toss: Keep It, Modernize It, or Skip It

| Option | Who It Works For | How to Execute |
|---|---|---|
| **Keep it (traditional)** | Couples who love the tradition, crowds with enough single guests to make it fun | Announce clearly, bring energy, make it brief |
| **Modernize it** | Bouquet toss to all guests regardless of relationship status; anniversary dance instead | Inclusive, warm, works for diverse crowds |
| **Skip it entirely** | Couples who find it cringe-worthy or whose guest list is mostly married couples | Just do not do it — no announcement needed, no explanation required |

The bouquet toss became a staple in a different era. If it feels right for your crowd, do it with joy. If it feels awkward and forced, skip it without guilt.

### The Anniversary Dance Tradition

This is one of the most reliably moving reception moments and is underused by most couples.

**How it works:** The DJ invites all married couples onto the dance floor. As the song plays, the DJ asks couples married less than one year to sit, then less than five years, then ten, and so on — until the couple married the longest is the last one standing. That couple receives the bouquet and shares a moment.

This works for almost any crowd. It brings older guests to the floor, creates an organic emotional moment, and involves every married guest in the reception. It also gives your photographer a beautiful set of images.

### Guest Book Alternatives That People Actually Use

| Alternative | What It Is | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| **Recipe card book** | Guests write a recipe or food memory | People actually read it afterward |
| **Advice cards** | Guests fill out structured cards: "Our best marriage advice is..." | More meaningful than signatures |
| **Photo booth print & sign** | Guests take a strip and sign the duplicate copy | High participation, fun energy |
| **Vinyl record matting** | Guests sign a matte around a framed record | Decorative and displayable |
| **Puzzle** | Guests sign the back of puzzle pieces | Assembled and framed later |

Traditional guest books sit on a table and collect about 40% participation. Interactive alternatives routinely get 80%+.

### Memory Table for Deceased Family Members

If you are creating a memory table or tribute display for family members who have passed:

- [ ] Place it in a visible but not trafficked area — the entrance or a quiet corner, not the center of the room
- [ ] Include photos, a small candle, and optionally a short note of tribute
- [ ] Brief your MC so they can acknowledge it briefly during opening remarks, then leave it in place without further call-outs
- [ ] Assign someone to ensure candles are extinguished at the end of the night
- [ ] Do not place the memory table near the bar or the dance floor

> **Reception Rule #7:** Tradition is a starting point, not a requirement. Every element of your reception should earn its place because it serves the couple or the guests — not because someone says it is what weddings do.

## 6.8 The Reception Winding Down

[Image: sparkler send-off lined path at night, guests holding sparklers forming a tunnel, couple running through the middle laughing]

### How to Signal the End Gracefully

The worst reception endings drift. The music gets quieter, people start saying awkward goodbyes, the bar closes mid-conversation, and no one is quite sure when to leave.

A graceful ending has a clear signal. The recommended sequence:

1. Last call announced 30 minutes before end time
2. MC makes a warm, enthusiastic announcement: *"We are coming up on the last stretch of the evening — get on that dance floor."*
3. One final slow song — announced by name and as the last dance
4. Lights transition (slightly brighter) to signal winding down
5. Guests guided toward the send-off

### The Grand Exit: Options and Logistics

| Exit Style | What You Need | Safety Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Sparklers** | Sparklers (36" minimum), long lighters, a coordinator to manage lighting sequence | Never use in enclosed spaces; check venue policy; designate a bucket of sand |
| **Bubbles** | Bubble wands for each guest, someone to distribute them | Slippery if on tile or hardwood — check your surface |
| **Ribbon Wands** | Pre-made ribbon wands, someone to distribute them | No fire risk, works indoors, lower visual drama |
| **Flower Petals** | Fresh or dried petals, distribution logistics | Beautiful photos; fresh petals can stain; confirm with venue |

- [ ] Designate one person (coordinator or trusted friend) to manage the send-off logistics
- [ ] Gather guests for the send-off line before the couple exits — not during
- [ ] Tell your photographer the send-off time at least 20 minutes in advance
- [ ] Practice lighting sparklers before the night if you have never done it — it takes longer than you think

### The After-Party Question

If you are hosting an after-party, communicate it clearly on the wedding website and in day-of communications. Do not assume word of mouth will work.

If you are not hosting one but guests will likely organize something themselves, you do not need to announce that. Just wish everyone well at the exit.

**What to never do:** Promise an after-party and then not have one, or have one that half the wedding party knows about and half does not. This creates hurt feelings and confusion.

### Venue Checkout: The End-of-Night Logistics

- [ ] Assign one non-drinking, responsible person to manage end-of-night venue checkout
- [ ] Create an itemized list of everything that needs to leave with you: gifts, cards, topper, rentals, personal decor
- [ ] Confirm with your venue what they handle (florals, linens) vs. what you retrieve
- [ ] Collect the card box before the venue empties — this is the most commonly forgotten item
- [ ] Confirm the final vendor payment schedule — some vendors collect their final balance at the end of the night
- [ ] Designate a car or vehicle for gifts and personal items so they have a place to go

> **Reception Rule #8:** A great ending feels intentional, not exhausted. Plan the last 45 minutes with the same care you gave the first 45.

## 6.9 The Toast Briefing Template

[Image: close-up of a champagne glass held up at a wedding reception, bokeh lights in the background, warm gold tones]

This is the exact document to send — via email or text — to every person giving a toast at your reception. Send it no later than three weeks before the wedding. Follow up once.

---

### TOAST BRIEFING — From [Couple Names]

**To:** [Toast-Giver Name]

**Re:** Your Toast at Our Wedding — [Date]

We are so honored that you will be sharing a few words at our reception. We know you are going to be amazing. To help you feel confident and prepared, here is everything you need to know.

---

**THE STRUCTURE WE RECOMMEND**

A great wedding toast has three parts:

1. **Who you are and how you know us** (1–2 sentences)
2. **One specific story or memory** that illustrates something true about one of us, or our relationship (60–90 seconds)
3. **A sincere closing wish or toast** that raises the glass (30 seconds)

That is it. Short, warm, specific, sincere. The best toasts ever given at weddings follow this structure.

---

**TIME LIMIT**

**3 minutes maximum. Ideally 2–2.5 minutes.**

Practice with a timer. Seriously — set a timer on your phone and read your toast aloud. What feels short when writing often runs perfectly long when spoken.

If your draft runs over 3 minutes, cut the second story. Keep the best one.

---

**WHAT NOT TO SAY**

We know you would never intentionally cause discomfort, but just to make it easy:

- [ ] No ex-partner references of any kind
- [ ] No inside jokes that require explanation to work
- [ ] No roast material that depends on "you had to be there"
- [ ] No references to past relationships, one-night stands, or hookup history
- [ ] No references to excessive drinking, drug use, or criminal history — even if funny
- [ ] No backhanded compliments ("I never thought [Name] would settle down, but...")
- [ ] No mention of how long it took us to get engaged or pressure around kids
- [ ] No crying through the entire toast without being able to speak — take a breath, take a moment, keep going

---

**HOW TO PRACTICE**

1. Write your toast in full — every word
2. Read it aloud three times
3. Time it on the third read
4. Memorize the general shape, not word for word (you will sound more natural)
5. Hold notes or a phone with your script — this is 100% acceptable and professional

---

**LOGISTICS ON THE NIGHT**

- The microphone will be [handheld / at a podium / passed to you by the MC]
- You will be introduced by name by the MC — listen for your name
- Stand up straight, speak slowly (nerves make us rush), and make eye contact with us, then with the room
- When you are done, [hand the microphone to the MC / place it back on the podium / pass it to the next speaker]
- Raise your glass and say your toast clearly — wait for the room to catch up before drinking

**ONE FINAL NOTE**

We are going to be sitting right in front of you, and we are going to be looking at you with full hearts. You cannot say the wrong thing if you speak from love. We trust you completely.

Thank you for being part of our day.

**With love,**
[Couple Names]
