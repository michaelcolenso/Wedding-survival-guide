## Appendix B: Vendor Tip Reference Guide

Tipping is not legally required, but it is a genuine acknowledgment of people who worked hard to make your day exceptional. Use this table as a starting point. Adjust based on the quality of service, how far vendors traveled, and how much they exceeded expectations.

| Vendor | Suggested Tip Range | When to Tip | Who Presents It | Etiquette Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Wedding planner / coordinator | $100–$500+ (or 10–20% of fee for smaller planners) | After the event | Couple or parent | Not always expected if they own the business; more common for employees or day-of coordinators |
| Officiant | $50–$200 | Day-of, after ceremony | Best man or MOH | If officiant is clergy, a donation to their organization is appropriate |
| Photographer | $50–$200 per shooter | After delivery of photos or end of night | Envelope in payment | If there is a second shooter, tip them separately ($50–$100) |
| Videographer | $50–$200 | End of night or at delivery | Envelope in payment | Same as photographer — acknowledge second operators |
| DJ | $50–$200 | End of night | Best man or planner | Tip the DJ, not the booking agency |
| Band | $25–$50 per musician | End of night | Planner or designated person | A band of 8 adds up fast — budget accordingly |
| Catering staff / servers | $20–$50 per server | End of night | Catering manager distributes | Many catering contracts include an 18–22% service charge — confirm before double-tipping |
| Bartenders | $50–$100 per bartender | End of night | Catering manager or planner | If guests are tipping directly, add-on tip is still appreciated |
| Hair stylist | 15–20% of service cost | Day-of, at end of service | Each wedding party member tips their own stylist | If one stylist did the whole party, the couple tips the total |
| Makeup artist | 15–20% of service cost | Day-of, at end of service | Same as above | |
| Florist | $50–$200 (for lead designer), $20–$50 per delivery/setup person | Day-of, at delivery | Planner or designated person | Not always expected; tip if setup was extensive or exceptional |
| Officiant (civil) | $50–$100 | Day-of | Best man or planner | |
| Transportation / drivers | 15–20% of total cost | At end of service each leg | MOH or best man | Tip each driver separately if using multiple vehicles |
| Venue coordinator | $50–$200 | End of night | Couple or planner | Only if they are venue employees, not a hired planner |
| Cake / baker delivery | $10–$25 per person | At delivery | Planner | Only for delivery and setup, not the baker themselves unless they came on-site |

**Key rule:** Separate the tip envelopes before the wedding. Label each one. Give them to your planner or the designated tip distributor so you do not have to think about it on the day.

**Service charge is not a tip.** If your catering contract includes an 18–22% service charge, that money goes to the catering company — not necessarily to the staff. Ask your caterer directly how service charges are distributed. If staff do not receive it, budget for additional gratuity.

## Appendix C: Wedding Insurance — Do You Need It?

Short answer: if you are spending more than $10,000 on a wedding, yes. Almost certainly yes.

### What Wedding Insurance Covers

**Vendor cancellation or failure:** If a vendor goes out of business, cancels without a replacement, or fails to show, wedding insurance can reimburse your non-refundable costs. This includes photographers, caterers, florists, and venues.

**Weather-related cancellation or postponement:** If a natural disaster, named storm, or extreme weather event forces a postponement, covered policies will help cover the costs to reschedule.

**Vendor no-delivery:** If your wedding dress is not delivered in time, or a vendor fails to deliver a paid product, your policy may cover the cost to replace it.

**Liability coverage:** If a guest is injured at your event, or property is damaged, liability insurance covers your legal exposure. This is the category many venues now require.

**Illness or injury:** If the couple or an immediate family member experiences a serious illness or injury close to the wedding date that forces cancellation, covered policies reimburse non-refundable deposits.

**Military deployment:** If a partner is unexpectedly deployed, most policies cover the costs of postponement.

### What Wedding Insurance Does NOT Cover

- Cold feet, a change of heart, or one partner deciding not to get married
- "I just changed my mind about the venue"
- Pre-existing conditions that were diagnosed before the policy was purchased
- Events outside the policy window (you need to buy insurance at booking, not the week before)
- Vendor disputes that are not a complete failure of service (wrong flowers are a contract dispute, not an insurance claim)

### Cost Reference

| Wedding Size / Budget | Estimated Coverage Cost |
|---|---|
| Small (under $10K) | $75–$200 |
| Mid-range ($10K–$30K) | $200–$400 |
| Large ($30K–$75K) | $400–$600 |
| Luxury ($75K+) | $600–$1,500+ |

Liability coverage (often sold separately) runs $185–$500 depending on guest count and alcohol service.

### When to Buy

Buy at your first major vendor deposit. Not the week before the wedding. Most policies will not cover vendor failure for vendors booked after the policy purchase date.

### Venues and Liability Insurance

As of 2026, many venues — particularly those that are not hotel properties — require couples to carry event liability insurance with the venue named as an additional insured. This is separate from your wedding cancellation policy. Read your venue contract carefully and look for this requirement before signing.

Recommended providers: WedSafe, Markel, Travelers, and USAA (for military families). Compare policies on coverage details, not just price.

## Appendix D: The Name Change Checklist

The legal name change process is sequential, not simultaneous. You cannot update your passport until you update your Social Security record. You cannot update your bank accounts until you have an updated ID. Follow this order to avoid making extra trips.

**Note:** Name change after marriage is entirely optional. Both partners can keep their names, hyphenate, or choose a new name entirely. This checklist applies to whoever is changing their name. In same-sex marriages, both partners may go through this process.

[Image: A tidy desk with a checklist, a pen, and a few official-looking documents — organized and calm, not stressful]

### Step 1: Social Security Administration
**Timeline:** 1–2 weeks for updated Social Security card to arrive
- [ ] Complete Form SS-5 (available at SSA.gov)
- [ ] Bring certified marriage certificate (not a photocopy)
- [ ] Bring current government-issued photo ID
- [ ] Visit your local SSA office in person — online update is not currently available for name change
- [ ] No fee required

### Step 2: State Driver's License or State ID
**Timeline:** Same day (new card issued or mailed within 2 weeks depending on state)
- [ ] Wait until your Social Security record is updated
- [ ] Bring your new or updated Social Security card confirmation
- [ ] Bring your certified marriage certificate
- [ ] Bring your current license
- [ ] Visit your state DMV in person
- [ ] Fee: typically $10–$35

### Step 3: Passport
**Timeline:** 6–8 weeks standard; 2–3 weeks expedited (fees apply)
- [ ] Complete Form DS-5504 if your current passport is less than 1 year old (no fee)
- [ ] Complete Form DS-82 if your passport was issued within the last 15 years
- [ ] Complete Form DS-11 if you need a brand-new passport
- [ ] Bring certified marriage certificate
- [ ] Bring current passport
- [ ] Bring new driver's license as secondary ID
- [ ] Submit by mail or at a passport acceptance facility
- [ ] Do not book international travel for 8+ weeks after submission unless you pay for expedited processing

### Step 4: Financial Accounts
**Timeline:** 1–4 weeks depending on institution
- [ ] Bank checking and savings accounts — visit branch in person with marriage certificate and new ID
- [ ] Investment accounts (brokerage, 401k, IRA) — call or visit
- [ ] Credit cards — most can be updated by phone or online with documentation
- [ ] Update beneficiary designations while you are making these calls

### Step 5: Employer HR and Payroll
**Timeline:** 1–2 pay cycles
- [ ] Notify HR department with a copy of your marriage certificate
- [ ] Update your W-4 if tax filing status is also changing
- [ ] Update your employee benefits (health insurance, life insurance, 401k beneficiaries)
- [ ] Update your work email if your organization uses name-based email addresses

### Step 6: Insurance Policies
**Timeline:** 1–2 weeks
- [ ] Health insurance — through employer HR or direct with insurer
- [ ] Auto insurance — call your insurer; may require updated license copy
- [ ] Life insurance — call your insurer; update beneficiaries at the same time
- [ ] Homeowner's or renter's insurance

### Step 7: Voter Registration
**Timeline:** Registration update typically processed within 2–4 weeks
- [ ] Update online at vote.gov or your state's voter registration portal
- [ ] Or update in person at your county election office
- [ ] Check registration status before any upcoming election

### Step 8: Professional Licenses and Certifications
**Timeline:** Varies by licensing board (2 weeks to several months)
- [ ] Medical, legal, teaching, real estate, financial, or nursing licenses
- [ ] Each licensing board has its own process — check their website directly
- [ ] Some require a court order or certified marriage certificate

### Step 9: Subscriptions and Online Accounts
**Timeline:** Ongoing — do this over a few weeks
- [ ] Email addresses and accounts
- [ ] Social media profiles
- [ ] Amazon, subscription boxes, streaming services
- [ ] Frequent flyer / hotel loyalty programs (must match passport for travel)
- [ ] Memberships (gym, library, professional organizations)

**Total timeline from marriage to fully updated:** Expect 2–4 months if done in order. Do not try to do it all in one week — the sequential dependencies will slow you down regardless.

## Appendix E: Glossary of Wedding Terms

Vendors and venues use terms that sound simple but have real legal and financial implications. Know what you are agreeing to before you sign.

**Attrition clause:** A contract provision requiring you to meet a minimum guaranteed spend (usually in food and beverage or room block). If you fall short, you owe the difference or a penalty. Common in hotel contracts. Negotiate this number down before signing.

**Banquet event order (BEO):** The venue's internal document that specifies every detail of your event — room setup, menu, timeline, staffing, and costs. Review and sign this 30 days before your wedding. It supersedes verbal agreements.

**Booking fee / retainer:** An upfront payment to hold your date, often 25–50% of the total. This is typically non-refundable. Do not confuse it with a "deposit" that implies refundability.

**Bustle:** A method of looping or pinning a wedding dress train up and out of the way for the reception. American bustle (loops on top), French bustle (buttons underneath), and Austrian bustle are different styles. Your seamstress will create and show you yours.

**Caterer, in-house vs. preferred vs. outside:** An in-house caterer is the only option allowed. A preferred caterer is on the venue's approved list, often for good reason. An outside caterer is a vendor you bring in independently, sometimes for an additional venue fee.

**Cocktail attire:** A dress code signaling knee-length or midi dresses for women, suits or blazers for men. Not black tie. Not casual.

**Day-of coordinator:** A professional who manages the logistics of your wedding day based on a plan you created — usually onboarding 30–60 days before the wedding. Different from a full-service planner who is involved from the beginning.

**Deposit:** A partial upfront payment. Unlike a retainer, a deposit may be partially refundable depending on the contract. Read the refund terms carefully — do not assume.

**Dress code, suggested vs. required:** Suggested dress codes are guidance. Required dress codes (as in the venue enforces them) are rare but exist at some private clubs and religious venues.

**Escrow:** Holding funds with a neutral third party until services are delivered. Rarely used in wedding contracts but worth requesting for high-value, high-risk bookings.

**First look:** A planned, private moment between the couple before the ceremony where they see each other for the first time, coordinated and photographed. Allows for couple portraits before the ceremony. Not everyone does one — it is a personal choice.

**Flat fee vs. percentage fee:** A flat fee is a fixed price regardless of total spend. A percentage fee grows with your budget. Full-service planners often charge 10–20% of the wedding budget. Understand which model your vendor uses.

**Floor plan:** A to-scale diagram of your venue layout showing table placement, dance floor, bar location, buffet, DJ/band placement, and ceremony setup. Your venue coordinator creates this. Review and approve it 30 days out.

**Force majeure:** A contract clause excusing performance due to extraordinary events outside a party's control — natural disasters, government shutdowns, pandemics. Read how your vendor defines this. Not all force majeure clauses are equal.

**Golden hour:** The hour after sunrise or before sunset when natural light is soft, warm, and flattering. Photographers often plan outdoor portraits around this window.

**Gratuity vs. service charge:** A gratuity is a voluntary tip given directly to staff. A service charge is a mandatory fee added to your bill by the vendor or venue. Service charges do not always go to the workers who served you. Ask.

**Greenery / foliage:** Leaves, vines, and non-floral plant material used in arrangements. Eucalyptus, ferns, and ivy are common. Often used to add volume to arrangements at lower cost than flowers.

**Guest count, estimated vs. guaranteed:** The estimated count is your working number during planning. The guaranteed count is the final number you commit to (usually 5–14 days before the wedding), and it is the number you are billed for — even if fewer guests attend. Never guarantee more than you expect.

**Head table vs. sweetheart table:** A head table seats the entire wedding party together facing the guests. A sweetheart table seats only the couple at a private table for two. Both are common; choose what feels right.

**Intimate wedding:** Generally defined as 50 guests or fewer. No universal standard — it is a marketing term.

**Liability waiver:** A contract provision in which you agree not to hold the vendor liable for certain types of loss. Read these carefully — some are reasonable (they are not liable for weather), some are not (they are not liable for losing your photos).

**Linen rental:** Table linens are almost always rental items, not purchase items. They come in standard sizing — confirm that the rental linens match your table dimensions. Floor-length vs. standard-length linens create very different looks.

**Micro wedding:** Generally defined as 20 guests or fewer. Gained popularity post-2020. Still requires most of the same vendor categories as a traditional wedding.

**Off-peak / off-season pricing:** Vendors often offer reduced rates for Sundays, Fridays, weekday weddings, or winter months. If your date is flexible, this is where significant savings are available.

**On consumption vs. per person pricing:** On consumption means you pay for what guests actually drink. Per person means you pay a flat rate per guest regardless of consumption. For light-drinking crowds, on-consumption pricing can save money.

**Officiant, legal requirements:** In most U.S. states, officiants must be ordained or licensed by a recognized religious or civil body. Online ordination (via Universal Life Church or similar) is legally valid in most states but not all. Verify in your state before having a friend officiate.

**Package pricing:** A bundled price for multiple services or items. Always get an itemized breakdown so you know what is actually included — and what is not.

**Preferred vendor list:** A venue's list of vendors they have worked with and recommend. Often a signal of quality, but sometimes also a revenue relationship. Ask whether the venue receives a referral fee from preferred vendors.

**Rain plan / Plan B:** A documented backup plan for outdoor events in case of weather. Every outdoor wedding needs one. If your venue does not have a rain plan, that is a red flag.

**Run of show:** A minute-by-minute timeline of your entire wedding day shared with all vendors. Your planner or coordinator creates and distributes this. The more detailed, the better.

**Seating chart vs. assigned seats:** A seating chart assigns guests to a table. Assigned seating assigns guests to a specific chair. Assigned seating reduces confusion and food service chaos. If you are doing plated dinners, assigned seating is strongly recommended.

**Service charge:** See "Gratuity vs. service charge."

**Shot list:** A list of specific photos you want captured — typically family groupings and must-have moments. Give this to your photographer at least two weeks before the wedding.

**Vendor meal:** Most vendor contracts include a requirement that you provide meals for vendors working the reception. This is standard and expected. Confirm the number of vendor meals with your caterer.

**Walk-through / site visit:** A pre-wedding visit to the venue with your planner and key vendors to review the layout, logistics, and final setup plan. Typically done 2–4 weeks before the wedding.
